The Memory Supercycle 2026–2028: Bridging the Structural Gap from Consumer to AI Dominance
Avril Wu
TrendForce Senior Research Vice President

The global memory market is currently undergoing a fundamental transformation that extends far beyond traditional cyclicality. Between 2025 and 2028, the industry is witnessing a "structural crunch" as leading manufacturers aggressively reallocate wafer capacity from commodity DRAM and NAND toward High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) to satisfy the insatiable appetite of AI infrastructure. This presentation analyzes the critical shift in Capital Expenditure (Capex) and capacity additions, exploring how the transition from consumer-driven demand to AI and general-purpose server dominance has created an unprecedented supply-demand imbalance. We will evaluate the impact of this transition on pricing and availability through 2028. Finally, we look toward the next horizon of production innovation, examining how emerging technologies like Compute Express Link (CXL) and High-Bandwidth Flash (HBF) will redefine the memory hierarchy and offer scalable solutions to the industry's capacity constraints.

14:20 – 14:40
NAND Flash Demand Projections in the Era of AI Inference
Bryan Ao
TrendForce Research Manager

As AI inference scales across cloud and edge environments, NAND Flash is experiencing a structural demand transformation. This session examines how the proliferation of large language models, vector databases, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems is reshaping storage requirements at the server level. We analyze the shift in enterprise SSD demand patterns driven by AI workloads, the divergence between consumer NAND pricing and enterprise SSD pricing dynamics, and how QLC adoption is accelerating to meet cost-per-bit requirements. The presentation will also explore regional supply dynamics, including NAND bit output forecasts through 2027 and the competitive positioning of major NAND suppliers as they navigate the transition from commodity storage toward AI-optimized solutions.

14:40 – 15:00
HBM Remains the Structural Anchor of the DRAM Industry Amid Shifting Profit Dynamics
Ellie Wang
TrendForce Senior Analyst

High-Bandwidth Memory has emerged as the single most consequential product category in the DRAM industry, reshaping supplier economics and capacity allocation decisions across the board. This session delivers an in-depth analysis of HBM3E supply-demand dynamics, the transition timeline toward HBM4, and the strategic divergence between Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron as each pursues differentiated technology roadmaps. We examine how HBM's margin premium is enabling DRAM suppliers to fund ongoing capital investment despite softness in standard DRAM segments, and evaluate scenarios for HBM pricing sustainability through 2028 as additional capacity comes online and competition intensifies for next-generation AI accelerator socket wins.

Navigating 2026 Foundry Market – Dual Realities of Advanced Dominance and Mature Saturation
Joanne Chiao
TrendForce Research Manager

The global foundry market in 2026 is defined by a stark divergence: while advanced nodes below 3nm experience robust AI-driven demand with limited supply, mature nodes above 28nm face chronic overcapacity driven by aggressive Chinese domestic capacity expansion. This presentation dissects both realities — analyzing TSMC's competitive moat and pricing power at leading-edge nodes, Intel Foundry's progress toward customer viability, and Samsung's efforts to stabilize yield at advanced nodes. Simultaneously, we examine the structural overcapacity dynamics in mature nodes, the pricing pressures affecting specialty foundries, and the geopolitical dimensions of domestic content requirements across key markets including China, the United States, and Europe.

15:40 – 16:00
AI Server Acceleration: From GPU Dominance to ASIC Diversification
Fion Chiu
TrendForce Analyst

The AI server market is undergoing its most significant architectural transformation since the initial GPU compute wave. As hyperscalers seek greater power efficiency, lower inference costs, and reduced dependency on a single supplier, custom silicon and merchant ASICs are rapidly gaining ground against GPU incumbents. This session analyzes the competitive landscape of AI accelerators including Google TPUs, AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia, and emerging merchant silicon providers. We evaluate the implications of ASIC diversification for server OEM supply chains, thermal and power delivery infrastructure requirements, and memory subsystem specifications — including rack-level power density trends and the growing role of liquid cooling in enabling next-generation AI compute density.
